Research on Human Activity Recognition and Assistance Systems

Hyunju Kim is a PhD candidate specializing in Human Activity Recognition and Assistance Systems. Her research centers on supporting users through distributed sensors and actuators, including a robot, while analyzing their activities based on sensory and behavioral time series.

She is dedicated to harnessing the power of artificial intelligence, machine learning and distributed IoT systems concepts to develop innovative systems capable of understanding and interpreting human activities, ultimately leading to smarter and more intuitive support systems. These technologies hold potential applications beyond smart spaces, extending to fields such as healthcare and surveillance systems.

Her ultimate goal is to conduct sustainable research that empowers users to lead productive lives, and she actively seeks interdisciplinary connections to gain a deeper understanding of users. If you are interested in working with me, please contact me at this Email.

Recent Publications

  • ART: Temporal relation embedded Transformer for the real world activity recognition

    AR-T employs the Transformer to discover long-term context correlations by computing attention scores between all context pairs. It also finds the importance weights of each context which makes infrequent but important contexts extracted as one of the activity patterns. To differentiate the same-type contexts with different duration, it embeds not only context type but also the duration and positional information as an input to the Transformer.

  • TAP: A transformer based activity prediction exploiting temporal relations in collaborative task

    TAP leverages Allen’s temporal relations to represent overlapping activities. However, an order of overlapping activities may not be directly correlated with their start times. This leads us to develop a model learning long-term correlations in (activity, temporal relation) pairs based on the Transformer. The model also enables finding infrequent but important activities that are not possible to derive in an LSTM layer.

  • ADELA: Attention based deep ensemble learning for activity recognition

    ADELA assigns a high score to important events with low frequency while a low score to continuously repetitive events, based on an attention score that measures the similarity between the hidden state of each event and the hidden state of its corresponding activity. By compressing the information of a sequence of events to a vector using the context vector of the attention mechanism, ADELA prevents the information loss between long-term related events.
